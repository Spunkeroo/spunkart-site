How to Build Your Artist Website for Free in 2026 — Complete Guide

This guide walks you through every step, from choosing your approach to going live with a polished, professional site that showcases your work and sells your art.

Why Every Artist Needs a Website

Social media is rented land. Instagram can change its algorithm tomorrow and cut your reach by 80%. Twitter can ban your account. TikTok can be removed from app stores. Your website is the only platform where you have complete control over how your work is presented, who sees it, and how people contact or buy from you.

A website also gives you:

  • Credibility. Galleries, clients, and collectors take you more seriously when you have a professional online presence beyond social media.
  • Discoverability. Google indexes websites, not Instagram posts. People searching for "abstract painter in Portland" or "commission digital artist" will find your website, not your social profile.
  • Direct sales. Sell prints, originals, commissions, and digital downloads without giving 20-50% to a marketplace. Your site, your rules, your margins.
  • Email list. Collect email addresses of people who love your work. Email is the most reliable way to reach your audience — no algorithm between you and your fans.
  • Professional inquiries. Galleries, publications, and brands search for artists online. A website with a clear contact page makes you findable for opportunities.

Three Approaches: Pick Your Path

There are three ways to build an artist website for free in 2026. Each has trade-offs:

Option 1: Website builders (Carrd, Cargo, WordPress.com). Easiest to set up. Drag and drop. Limited customization on free tiers. You may have the builder's branding on your site.

Option 2: GitHub Pages + HTML template. Completely free forever with no branding. Requires minimal technical knowledge (copy-paste level). Full control over every pixel. Read our GitHub Pages guide for setup instructions.

Option 3: AI-built custom site. Use an AI tool like Bolt.new or Claude Code to generate a completely custom website. Most flexible, best results, requires some comfort with technology. This is the approach we recommend for 2026.

Option 1: Website Builders (Easiest)

Carrd

Carrd creates beautiful single-page websites. Perfect for a simple portfolio with an about section, gallery, and contact form. The free tier includes 3 sites with a carrd.co subdomain. Pro ($19/year) removes branding and adds custom domains. For artists who want something live in 30 minutes, Carrd is the fastest option.

Try Carrd

Cargo

Cargo is designed specifically for creative portfolios. The templates are gallery-focused with beautiful typography and image presentation. The free tier includes 12 pages and a cargo.site subdomain. If your priority is making your images look stunning with minimal effort, Cargo is the best website builder for visual artists.

Try Cargo

WordPress.com

The free WordPress.com plan gives you a full website with blog, pages, and basic customization. Thousands of themes are available, many designed for portfolios. The limitations: wordpress.com subdomain, limited storage, and WordPress branding. Upgrade to Personal ($4/month) for custom domain and branding removal.

Try WordPress.com

Option 2: GitHub Pages + Template (Free Forever)

This is the approach we use for many of our own sites (including parts of SpunkArt.com). GitHub Pages gives you free hosting with HTTPS, a global CDN, and custom domain support. You upload HTML files to a repository, and GitHub serves them as a website. It costs nothing and will remain free indefinitely.

  1. Create a GitHub account at github.com (free).
  2. Create a new repository named yourusername.github.io.
  3. Choose a template. Download an artist portfolio HTML template from sites like HTML5 UP, Themefisher, or use our free website templates. All of these offer free, responsive portfolio templates.
  4. Customize the template. Replace placeholder images with your artwork. Edit the text in the HTML files (any text editor works). Replace the contact email with yours.
  5. Upload to GitHub. Push your files to the repository (GitHub Desktop makes this easy with no command line needed).
  6. Your site is live at yourusername.github.io within minutes.
  7. Add a custom domain (optional). Buy a domain ($10/year from Cloudflare) and configure it in your repository settings.

Pro Tip: Use an AI to Customize Your Template

Download a template, then use Claude or ChatGPT to customize it. Paste the HTML code and say "change the color scheme to dark blue and gold, add a section for commissions with a pricing table, and replace the placeholder text with copy for a watercolor landscape artist." The AI will modify the code for you. Copy it back into your files.

Building Your Portfolio Section

The portfolio is the most important section of your artist website. Here is how to do it right:

Image Quality and Optimization

Use high-resolution images but optimize them for web. A 5MB image takes forever to load on mobile. Use our image compression tools to reduce file sizes by 60-80% without visible quality loss. Target 200-400KB per image for gallery thumbnails and 500KB-1MB for full-size views. Use WebP format for the best quality-to-size ratio.

Gallery Layout

A masonry grid (like Pinterest's layout) works best for artwork of varying dimensions. It displays pieces at their natural aspect ratios without awkward cropping. Each image should open in a lightbox overlay with navigation arrows to browse through pieces. Include the title, medium, dimensions, year, and price (if for sale) under each piece.

Organization

If you work in multiple mediums or series, organize your portfolio with categories or filters. Let visitors click "Paintings," "Digital," "Commissions," or "Prints" to filter the gallery. This helps buyers find what they want and helps galleries understand your range.

What to Include

  • Your best 15-25 pieces. Quality over quantity. A portfolio of 15 stunning pieces is more impressive than 100 mediocre ones.
  • Recent work first. Your newest work should appear at the top. This shows you are active and growing.
  • Detail shots. For physical artwork, include close-up detail shots that show texture and technique.
  • Context shots. Show artwork in a room setting to help buyers visualize the piece in their space.

Adding an Online Store

Your website should sell your work, not just display it. Here are the best free and low-cost options for selling art online:

Gumroad (Digital Products and Prints)

Sell digital downloads (wallpapers, brushes, tutorials, digital art files) and physical products through Gumroad. Embed buy buttons directly on your website. Gumroad handles payments, delivery, and taxes. No monthly fee — they take 10% of each sale. Perfect for selling prints, digital art, or art courses.

Try Gumroad

Ko-fi Shop

Ko-fi has a free shop feature specifically designed for creators. Sell physical and digital products with no monthly fee and 0% commission on the free plan. They only charge the payment processing fee (PayPal or Stripe). Ko-fi also supports commissions, memberships, and tips. For artists, it is the most creator-friendly shop option.

Try Ko-fi

Print on Demand: Printful or Gelato

Upload your artwork, and these services print it on canvas, paper, apparel, phone cases, and more. They handle printing, packaging, and shipping. You set the retail price and keep the profit margin. No inventory, no upfront costs. Integrate directly with your website or sell through their storefront. Combine with our digital product selling guide.

Cost: Free to set up. You pay per order (deducted from customer payment).

Commission Page Setup

If you take commissions, dedicate a page to it. Include:

  • Pricing table with clear tiers (e.g., sketch $50, colored illustration $150, full painting $500).
  • Examples of commissioned work at each tier.
  • Process description: how many revisions, turnaround time, what you need from the client.
  • Current availability: whether your commissions are open or closed, and estimated wait time.
  • Commission form: a structured form so clients provide all the information you need upfront.

SEO for Artists

Search engine optimization helps people find your website when they search for art. Here are the SEO basics every artist should implement:

Title Tags and Meta Descriptions

Every page needs a unique title tag (the text that appears in browser tabs and search results). Format: "Your Name — Medium, Location | Artist Portfolio." Your meta description should be a compelling 150-character summary. Use our free SEO tools to check your tags.

Image Alt Text

Every artwork image needs descriptive alt text. This is how Google understands your images. Instead of "IMG_4521.jpg," use "Abstract acrylic painting with blue and gold tones on 36x48 canvas by [Your Name]." This helps your artwork appear in Google Image Search, which is a significant traffic source for artists.

Local SEO

If you sell locally or do in-person commissions, include your city and state on your website. "Digital portrait artist in Austin, Texas" helps you rank for local searches. Create a Google Business Profile (free) to appear in Google Maps results.

Blog Content

A simple blog with posts about your process, techniques, and inspiration helps SEO enormously. Each blog post is a new page that Google can index and rank. "How I Paint Watercolor Landscapes" or "My Process for Digital Portrait Commissions" attracts visitors who might become buyers. Post once or twice a month.

The Artist SEO Cheat Sheet

Include your name, medium, style, and location on every page. Add alt text to every image. Write blog posts about your process. Submit your site to Google Search Console. Link to your website from every social media profile. This alone puts you ahead of 90% of artists online. Read our 50 SEO tips for advanced strategies.

Getting a Custom Domain

A custom domain (yourname.com) costs about $10/year and makes your site look professional. Here is how to set it up:

  1. Choose your domain name. FirstNameLastName.com is the classic choice for artists. If it is taken, try adding "art" (janedoeart.com) or use a creative TLD (.art, .studio, .gallery).
  2. Buy from Cloudflare Registrar. Lowest prices, no markup, free DNS and SSL. A .com is about $10/year.
  3. Connect to your hosting. If using GitHub Pages, add a CNAME file to your repository and configure DNS records at Cloudflare. GitHub's documentation walks you through this in 5 minutes.
  4. Enable HTTPS. Both GitHub Pages and Cloudflare provide free SSL certificates. HTTPS is mandatory for professional websites and Google ranking.

Avoid These Domain Mistakes

Do not use hyphens (jane-doe-art.com looks unprofessional). Do not use numbers unless they are part of your brand. Do not buy from GoDaddy or similar registrars that charge inflated renewal prices. Do not skip the .com — if your name is available as a .com, always get it first, even if you plan to use a different TLD as your primary.

Launch Checklist

  1. Portfolio: 15-25 best pieces, optimized images, descriptions, lightbox gallery.
  2. About page: Bio, artist statement, professional photo, contact information.
  3. Contact page: Contact form or email address. Include commission inquiry form if applicable.
  4. Store or shop: At least one way to buy your work (Gumroad, Ko-fi, commission form).
  5. Mobile responsive: Test on your phone. Every element should be readable and navigable on mobile.
  6. Fast loading: All images optimized. Use our image tools to compress before uploading.
  7. SEO basics: Title tags, meta descriptions, alt text on all images, sitemap.
  8. Social links: Links to your Instagram, Twitter/X, and any other platforms.
  9. Email capture: A simple signup form for updates. Use Beehiiv or ConvertKit (both free).
  10. Analytics: Google Analytics or Plausible installed so you can see traffic data.
  11. Custom domain: (Optional but recommended) yourname.com connected and HTTPS enabled.
  12. Cross-linked: Update all social media bios with your website URL.
