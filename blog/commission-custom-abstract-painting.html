How to Commission a Custom Abstract Painting

A commissioned painting is created specifically for you. It is built around your space, your colors, your energy, and your vision. Unlike buying from a gallery, where you choose from existing work, a commission gives you a painting that exists nowhere else in the world — one that was made with your wall, your room, and your life in mind.

Commissioning art might sound like something reserved for the wealthy or the art-world elite. It is not. Independent artists work on commissions at every price point, and the process is more straightforward than you might expect. This guide walks you through every step, from finding the right artist to hanging the finished piece.

Why Commission a Custom Painting?

There are several compelling reasons to commission rather than buy existing work:

  • Perfect fit for your space. You choose the exact dimensions, orientation, and color palette to match your room. No more compromising on size or hoping a painting works with your existing decor.
  • Personal meaning. A commissioned piece can incorporate colors, themes, or emotions that are meaningful to you. It becomes a deeply personal object that reflects your story.
  • Artist collaboration. Working with an artist on a commission creates a relationship. You get to understand their process, see the work evolve, and own a piece with a story that is partly yours.
  • Guaranteed uniqueness. Every commission is created from scratch. There is no risk of seeing the same painting in someone else’s home or office.
  • Ideal gifting. A commissioned painting is one of the most thoughtful and impressive gifts you can give. It shows intentionality and care that no off-the-shelf purchase can match.

Finding the Right Artist

The most important decision in commissioning a painting is choosing the right artist. Here is how to find your match:

Study Their Existing Work

Browse the artist’s portfolio thoroughly. A commission should be an extension of the artist’s established style, not a departure from it. If you love an artist’s bold, textured, mixed media work, commission them to create more of that. Do not ask a color field painter to create gestural expressionism — you will both be frustrated.

Look for Consistency and Range

A strong portfolio shows both consistency (a recognizable voice and vision) and range (the ability to work across sizes, palettes, and moods). An artist who has created compelling work in various formats will adapt well to your specific requirements.

Check Their Process

Many artists share process videos, studio tours, and works-in-progress on social media. These give you insight into how they work and what the commission experience will feel like. An artist who communicates openly about their process will communicate openly during your commission.

Read Testimonials

If the artist has completed commissions before, look for testimonials or reviews from previous clients. Past commission success is the strongest predictor of future commission success.

Start with a Conversation

Reach out before committing. A brief conversation will tell you whether the artist understands your vision, communicates well, and feels like a good fit. Chemistry matters — you are entering a creative collaboration.

The Commission Process: Step by Step

While every artist has their own workflow, most abstract painting commissions follow a similar arc:

1 Initial Consultation

You share your vision: the space where the painting will hang (photos help enormously), preferred dimensions, color palette, mood/energy, and any specific references or inspirations. The artist asks questions to understand what you are looking for and whether the project is a good fit for their practice.

2 Proposal and Agreement

The artist provides a proposal that includes the scope (size, materials, estimated completion date), price, payment terms, and revision policy. Both parties agree before work begins. Most artists require a deposit (typically 50%) to secure the commission and cover material costs.

3 Creation

The artist creates the work. Many artists share progress photos during the process so you can see the painting evolve. Abstract commissions require trust in the artist’s creative process — the best results come when the artist has room to follow the painting where it leads, guided by your brief but not constrained by it.

4 Review

The artist shares the completed (or near-completed) work for your review. Most commission agreements include one round of minor revisions. For abstract work, revisions might include adjusting color intensity, adding or reducing texture in certain areas, or refining the balance of the composition.

5 Finishing and Delivery

Once approved, the artist applies a protective varnish, allows proper curing time, and carefully packages the painting for shipping or local pickup. The remaining balance is due before or upon delivery. You receive the painting along with a certificate of authenticity.

These are approximate ranges. The actual price depends on multiple factors:

  • Materials. Professional-grade acrylics, mixed media elements, specialty pigments, and premium canvas or panel add to material costs.
  • Complexity. A multi-layered mixed media painting with texture work takes significantly more time and materials than a single-layer color field piece.
  • Timeline. Rush commissions (faster than the artist’s standard timeline) often carry a premium of 25–50%.
  • Shipping. Large paintings require custom crating and careful shipping, which can add $100–$500+ depending on distance and size.

Value Tip

Commissions from emerging artists offer the best value in the art market. You get a custom, one-of-a-kind original from a talented artist at a fraction of what you would pay at a gallery for existing work by a mid-career or established name. And if the artist’s career develops, your early commission becomes increasingly valuable.

Writing an Effective Creative Brief

The quality of your commission starts with the quality of your brief. A good creative brief gives the artist clear direction while leaving room for creative freedom. Here is what to include:

The Space

Share photos of the room and the specific wall where the painting will hang. Include measurements of the available wall space. Note the lighting (natural vs. artificial, warm vs. cool). Mention any dominant colors in the room (flooring, furniture, curtains) that the painting should complement or contrast.

Size and Orientation

Specify your preferred dimensions (or range) and whether you want landscape (horizontal), portrait (vertical), or square orientation. If you are unsure, tape paper to the wall to visualize different sizes before deciding.

Color Palette

Provide specific colors you want included, excluded, or emphasized. Photos, paint swatches, or links to reference images are more useful than color names (what you call "teal" might be what the artist calls "turquoise"). Mention whether you want a warm, cool, or neutral palette overall.

Mood and Energy

Abstract art communicates through feeling, so describing the mood you want is essential. Is it calm and contemplative? Energetic and explosive? Moody and atmospheric? Words like "serene," "bold," "turbulent," "warm," "minimal," or "dense" help the artist calibrate the work’s emotional register.

References

Share images of the artist’s existing work that you love, or other paintings/photos that capture the feeling you are looking for. References are guides, not blueprints — the artist will interpret them through their own vision.

What to Avoid Saying

Do not micromanage the creative process. Avoid instructions like "put a red splash in the upper right corner" or "make it look exactly like this other painting." Trust the artist’s expertise. The most successful commissions give clear direction on the feeling and leave the execution to the professional.

Timelines and Expectations

A typical abstract painting commission takes 3–8 weeks from deposit to delivery. Here is a general breakdown:

  • Week 1: Consultation, agreement, deposit. Artist sources materials and prepares the surface.
  • Weeks 2–4: Primary creation phase. The artist works through layers, allowing proper drying time between sessions. Progress updates may be shared.
  • Week 5: Review, minor revisions if needed, final varnishing.
  • Week 6+: Curing time (varnish needs 2–7 days to cure properly), packaging, shipping or delivery.

Larger and more complex works may take longer. Multi-panel pieces, oversized canvases, and heavily textured mixed media work all require additional time. Discuss timeline expectations upfront and build in buffer for the unexpected — art is not a factory process, and the best results often come when the artist is not rushed.

What to Avoid

Commission pitfalls to watch for:

  1. Choosing an artist based on price alone. The cheapest option is rarely the best value. Look at the quality of their portfolio and the professionalism of their process. A $500 painting that falls apart in two years costs more than a $1,500 painting that lasts a lifetime.
  2. Skipping the written agreement. Always have a written agreement that covers scope, price, timeline, revision policy, and payment terms. No exceptions. Verbal agreements lead to misunderstandings.
  3. Expecting an exact copy. A commission is a new creation, not a reproduction. Even if you reference an existing work by the artist, the new piece will be different. Embrace that — it is what makes it yours.
  4. Giving conflicting direction. "I want it bold and subtle, warm and cool, structured and chaotic" is not helpful. Pick a clear direction and trust the artist to add nuance and complexity.
  5. Rushing the process. Art takes time. Pressuring an artist to rush almost always degrades the result. Plan ahead and give the artist the time they need to create their best work for you.

Red Flags

Be wary of artists who: refuse to provide references from previous commission clients, will not put the agreement in writing, demand full payment upfront (a 50% deposit is standard), or promise unrealistically fast turnaround times. A professional commission process is transparent, documented, and structured.
