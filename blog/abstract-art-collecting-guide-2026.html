Why Originals, Not Prints

Before we go further, one principle needs to be established: this guide is about collecting original art. Not prints, not reproductions, not limited editions. The case for originals is comprehensive — originals appreciate while prints depreciate, originals have physical texture and depth that prints cannot replicate, and originals carry the direct mark of the artist’s hand.

The most common misconception in art buying is that originals are prohibitively expensive. They are not. Original paintings by emerging artists — working painters who are actively producing, exhibiting, and building their careers — are available at prices that overlap with "premium" prints. The difference is that the original will gain value over time while the print will lose value. Read the full case for originals-only to understand why this distinction matters so much.

"The best time to buy original art is before the artist becomes famous. The second best time is now."

How to Find Emerging Artists

The traditional art world funnels attention toward a small number of gallery-represented artists. But in 2026, the best art buying opportunities are often outside that system. Emerging artists who sell directly to collectors offer better prices, more accessible relationships, and stronger potential for value appreciation.

Where to Look

  • Social media (especially X and Instagram). Most working artists post their work on social platforms. Follow hashtags like #abstractart, #originalart, #acrylicpainting, and #contemporaryart. Look for artists who show process photos and in-progress work — this is a sign of a genuinely producing painter, not someone reselling prints. Follow @SpunkArt13 for an example of an active, producing artist.
  • Artist websites. Artists with their own websites (not just marketplace profiles) tend to be more serious about their practice. Look for galleries showing a substantial body of work, information about materials and process, and clear purchasing options.
  • Local art shows and open studios. Nothing replaces seeing art in person. Local art walks, open studio events, and community gallery shows are where you find emerging artists before the market discovers them. The Chicago area, for instance, has a thriving scene of independent artists showing in non-traditional spaces.
  • Art fairs. Regional and emerging art fairs (not the blue-chip mega-fairs) are curated collections of working artists at accessible price points. They let you see a lot of work quickly and talk directly with artists.
  • Online art communities. Reddit communities like r/AbstractArt, Discord servers for art collectors, and Substack newsletters from art critics and curators are all pipelines to discovering emerging talent.

Pro Tip

When you find an artist whose work you respond to, follow their social media and their website for several months before buying. Watch how their work evolves. See if they produce consistently. Artists who post new work regularly are actively developing — and active development is the single best predictor of career growth and value appreciation.

How to Evaluate Abstract Art

Abstract art does not depict recognizable subjects, which makes some buyers unsure how to evaluate it. But there are concrete criteria that separate strong abstract work from weak abstract work. You do not need an art degree to apply them.

Composition

Does the painting feel balanced? Does your eye move through the piece or get stuck in one spot? Strong abstract composition creates visual flow — the eye enters the painting, travels across it guided by color and form, and finds resting points before moving again. Weak composition feels random or dead.

Color Relationships

Do the colors work together? Do they create energy, tension, harmony, or mood? A skilled abstract painter controls color relationships with the same precision a musician controls pitch. The colors should feel intentional, not accidental. Look for evidence of color theory in action: complementary contrasts, temperature shifts, value range.

Texture and Surface

An original painting should have physical presence. Look for evidence of process: visible brush marks, palette knife texture, layering, depth. The surface of a well-made abstract painting is rich with information. If the surface looks thin, flat, or unworked, the painting may not have the physical substance that makes originals valuable.

Materials

Ask the artist what materials they use. Premium materials — artist-grade paints, professional canvases, archival mediums — are a sign of a serious painter who cares about the longevity of their work. Artists who use student-grade materials are not investing in the durability of the work they are selling you.

Emotional Response

After all the technical evaluation, the most important criterion is subjective: does the painting make you feel something? Abstract art communicates through color, form, and energy rather than narrative. If a painting arrests your attention, stirs an emotion, or makes you want to keep looking — trust that response. That is the painting doing its job.

What Drives Pricing

  • Size. Larger paintings use more materials and take more time. Price generally scales with surface area.
  • Materials. Artist-grade paints and professional canvases cost significantly more than budget alternatives. Artists who invest in premium materials will price accordingly.
  • Time and complexity. A painting built in 30 layers over three weeks costs more to produce than a painting completed in one session. Complexity, detail, and layering are reflected in price.
  • Career stage. Emerging artists price lower than established artists. This is exactly why buying from emerging artists is such a strong value proposition — you are getting serious work at pre-career-growth prices.
  • Sales channel. Gallery-represented artists add a 50% gallery commission to their prices. Buying directly from the artist eliminates this markup entirely.

Buying Direct vs. Gallery

In the traditional art world, galleries serve as intermediaries between artists and collectors. They curate, exhibit, promote, and sell. In exchange, they take a commission of 40–60% on every sale. This model has worked for decades, but it also means that when you buy a $2,000 painting from a gallery, $800–$1,200 goes to the gallery and $800–$1,200 goes to the artist.

Buying directly from the artist flips this equation. Your entire payment goes to the person who created the work. The artist earns more per painting, which supports their continued practice. You pay a fair price without the gallery markup. And you build a direct relationship with the maker — a relationship that can include studio visits, first access to new work, and the ability to commission custom pieces.

When Galleries Add Value

Galleries are not without merit. They provide curation (filtering thousands of artists down to a curated roster), exhibition space (seeing art in person under proper lighting), and validation (a gallery's reputation endorses the artists they represent). If you prefer a curated experience and do not mind paying the premium, gallery buying is a legitimate path.

But for collectors who want maximum value per dollar and a direct connection to the artist, buying direct is the superior approach in 2026. The internet has eliminated the discovery problem that galleries once solved. You can find, research, and evaluate artists entirely online, then buy directly from their website or through a DM conversation.

How to Commission a Custom Piece

Commissioning a painting means working with an artist to create an original piece tailored to your specific space, palette, size, and vision. It is the most personalized way to acquire art, and it results in a painting that was made specifically for you and your walls.

The full commissioning guide covers the process in detail, but here is the overview:

1 Initial Conversation

You share your space, preferred colors, desired size, and any visual references or mood you are aiming for. The artist asks questions and establishes whether the project is a good fit.

2 Proposal and Pricing

The artist provides a proposal with size, material specifications, timeline, and price. Commissions are typically priced 10–30% above the artist’s standard pricing for comparable-sized work because the creation is bespoke.

3 Creation

The artist creates the painting. Depending on the arrangement, they may share progress photos at key stages. Abstract commissions typically allow the artist creative freedom within the agreed parameters — you define the boundaries, the artist fills them.

4 Delivery

The finished painting is photographed, cured, varnished, and shipped or delivered. Gallery-depth canvases arrive ready to hang with no framing needed.

Your First Purchase: A Step-by-Step Guide

If you have never bought original art before, the process can feel intimidating. It should not be. Here is the step-by-step path from "I like that painting" to "that painting is on my wall."

  1. Notice what you respond to. Spend time looking at art online and in person. Pay attention to which pieces stop you. Color, energy, texture, mood — what pulls you in?
  2. Follow the artist. Once you find an artist whose work you consistently respond to, follow them on social media and visit their website. Watch their practice over time.
  3. Decide on size and budget. Measure the wall where you want to hang the painting. Set a realistic budget. Remember that a $500 original is a better investment than five $100 prints.
  4. Reach out to the artist. Most artists are approachable and grateful for genuine collector interest. Send a message expressing interest in a specific piece or asking about commissions. Be direct and specific.
  5. Ask about materials. Confirm that the painting is made with professional-grade, archival materials. This protects your investment.
  6. Make the purchase. Complete the transaction through the artist’s website or agreed payment method. Get confirmation of the details: size, materials, delivery timeline.
  7. Hang it and live with it. The best part. Put the painting on your wall and watch how it changes your space. Notice how different lighting reveals different qualities. This is what original art does that prints cannot.

Red Flags to Watch For

Red Flags When Buying Art

Watch out for these signs that something may not be what it seems.

  • No information about materials. If an artist cannot or will not tell you what paints and canvas they use, that is a concern. Serious artists know their materials and are happy to discuss them.
  • "Limited edition originals." This phrase is a contradiction. An original is one of one. If the artist makes multiple similar versions of the same composition, those are variations, not originals in the true 1-of-1 sense.
  • AI-generated art sold as "original paintings." In 2026, this is an increasing issue. Ask for process photos showing the painting in progress on a physical easel. A real painting has a creation story that AI-generated images do not.
  • Unusually low prices for large work. A 36x48 original painting requires $50–$150 in materials alone, plus days of labor. If a large painting is priced at $50, something is wrong — either the materials are extremely poor quality, or the work is not what it claims to be.
  • No body of work. An artist with three paintings on their website and no history of exhibitions, shows, or consistent production is not yet established enough to buy with confidence. Look for artists with substantial, developed bodies of work — dozens or hundreds of pieces that show progression.
  • Pressure tactics. "This painting will be gone tomorrow" and "buy now or miss out" are sales tactics, not art. A legitimate artist will give you time to make a considered decision.

Caring for Your Collection

Original paintings require minimal maintenance, but proper care ensures they last generations.

  • Avoid direct sunlight. Even with UV-resistant varnish, prolonged direct UV exposure can affect colors over decades. Hang paintings on walls that receive indirect or filtered light.
  • Avoid humidity extremes. Canvas expands and contracts with humidity changes. Avoid hanging paintings in bathrooms, above stoves, or on uninsulated exterior walls. Maintain consistent indoor humidity between 40–60%.
  • Dust gently. Use a soft, dry microfiber cloth or a clean soft brush to remove surface dust. Do not use chemical cleaners, water, or compressed air directly on the painting surface.
  • Handle with care. When moving paintings, hold them by the stretcher bar edges, not by the canvas surface. Wear clean cotton gloves to avoid transferring oils from your skin to the paint film.
  • Insure your collection. Once your collection reaches significant value, get an art insurance rider on your homeowner’s policy. Document each painting with high-resolution photographs and purchase receipts.

Art as Investment: Realistic Expectations

Original art from emerging artists has historically appreciated at 5–10% annually when the artist’s career progresses. But art should never be purchased as a pure financial instrument. The primary return on art is the daily experience of living with something beautiful, meaningful, and singular on your walls. The financial appreciation is a bonus, not the purpose.

That said, buying smart matters. The factors that predict appreciation are:

  • Consistent production. Artists who continue to produce at a high level build larger bodies of work, which builds market depth and collector interest. An artist with 300+ paintings has demonstrated sustained commitment.
  • Growing visibility. Active social media presence, press coverage, exhibition history, and collector base growth all indicate an artist whose career is on an upward trajectory.
  • Material quality. Paintings made with archival materials maintain their physical condition, which preserves their market value. Deteriorating paintings lose value rapidly.
  • Originals-only practice. Artists who refuse to sell prints protect the scarcity value of every original in their body of work. Your painting cannot be devalued by reproductions entering the market.
  • Direct sales. Buying directly from the artist at pre-gallery pricing means your acquisition cost is lower, which means the threshold for appreciation is lower.
